What is AI?

AI is computer science focused on creating smart machines that mimic human intelligence. They perceive, process data, learn, reason, solve problems, make decisions, and take actions. Machine Learning and Deep Learning are key.

History of AI

Artificial intelligence traces back to ancient Greek philosophers who built automatons—mechanical devices capable of independent movement. Notable examples include a mechanical pigeon from 400 BCE and Leonardo da Vinci's creations in the Renaissance. Our site delves into the 20th century, highlighting the crucial advancements by engineers and scientists towards modern AI.

Groundwork for AI: 1900-1950

In the early 1900s, media focused on artificial humans, prompting scientists to explore the possibility of creating an artificial brain. Some creators created simple robots, which were mostly steam-powered and capable of making facial expressions and walking. The term "robot" was coined in a Czech play in 1921.

  • 1921: Czech playwright Karel Čapek introduced the concept of "artificial people" in his science fiction play "Rossum's Universal Robots."
  • 1929: Japanese professor Makoto Nishimura built the first Japanese robot, Gakutensoku.
  • 1949: Computer scientist Edmund Callis Berkley published "Giant Brains, or Machines that Think," comparing computers to human brains.

Birth of AI: 1950-1956

The interest in AI surged during this period when Alan Turing's "Computer Machinery and Intelligence" led to the creation of The Turing Test, a tool used to measure computer intelligence, coining the term "artificial intelligence."

  • 1950: Alan Turing published "Computer Machinery and Intelligence," which introduced the Imitation Game as a test of machine intelligence.
  • 1952: Arthur Samuel developed the first program to learn checkers independently.
  • 1955: John McCarthy held the first workshop on "artificial intelligence," marking its popular usage.

AI Maturation: 1957-1979

The period between the creation of the term "artificial intelligence" and the 1980s was marked by rapid growth and struggle for AI research. The late 1950s through the 1960s saw the development of programming languages and books that explored robots. The 1970s saw advancements like the first anthropomorphic robot in Japan and the first autonomous vehicle. However, the U.S. government showed little interest in funding AI research.

  • 1958: John McCarthy created LISP, the first programming language for AI research.
  • 1959: Arthur Samuel coined the term "machine learning" to teach machines to play chess better than humans.
  • 1961: The first industrial robot, Unimate, began working on an assembly line at General Motors.
  • 1965: Edward Feigenbaum and Joshua Lederberg created the first "expert system."
  • 1966: Joseph Weizenbaum created the first chatbot ELIZA.
  • 1968: Soviet mathematician Alexey Ivakhnenko proposed a new approach to AI, later known as "Deep Learning."
  • 1973: James Lighthill reported to the British Science Council about the slow progress in AI research.
  • 1979: James L. Adams created The Standford Cart, one of the first autonomous vehicles.
  • 1979: The American Association of Artificial Intelligence (now AAAI) was founded.

AI Boom: 1980-1987

The 1980s saw a significant AI boom, fueled by research breakthroughs and government funding. Deep Learning techniques and Expert System usage gained popularity, enabling computers to learn from mistakes and make independent decisions, marking a significant period in AI development.

  • 1980: The AAAI held its first conference at Stanford.
  • 1980: XCON, an expert system, was introduced.
  • 1981: The Japanese government allocated $850 million to the Fifth Generation Computer project.
  • 1984: The AAAI warned of an upcoming "AI Winter."
  • 1985: AARON, an autonomous drawing program, was demonstrated.
  • 1986: The first driverless car was created.
  • 1987: Alacrity, a strategy managerial advisory system, was launched.

AI Winter: 1987-1993

An AI Winter has occurred due to low interest in AI, leading to decreased research funding and few breakthroughs. Private investors and the government halted funding due to high costs and low returns. This Winter was caused by setbacks in the machine market and expert systems, such as the end of the Fifth Generation project, strategic computing initiatives, and slow deployment of expert systems.

  • 1987: The market for specialized LISP-based hardware collapsed.
  • 1988: Rollo Carpenter invented the chatbot Jabberwacky.

AI Agents: 1993-2011

During the AI Winter, the early 90s saw significant advancements in AI research, including the first AI system capable of beating a world champion chess player, Roomba, and commercial speech recognition software on Windows computers. This era also introduced AI into everyday life, and a surge in interest led to increased funding for further research.

  • 1997: Deep Blue defeated world chess champion Gary Kasparov.
  • 1997: Windows released speech recognition software.
  • 2000: Professor Cynthia Breazeal created Kismet, the first robot to simulate human emotions.
  • 2002: Roomba was released.
  • 2006: Companies like Twitter, Facebook, and Netflix began using AI in advertising and UX algorithms.
  • 2010: Microsoft launched the Xbox 360 Kinect.
  • 2011: IBM's Watson won Jeopardy, and Apple introduced Siri.

Artificial General Intelligence: 2012-Present

Recent developments in AI have seen a surge in common-use tools like virtual assistants and search engines, popularizing Deep Learning and Big Data during this time period.

  • 2012: Google researchers trained a neural network to recognize cats.
  • 2015: Elon Musk, Stephen Hawking, and Steve Wozniak signed an open letter banning autonomous weapons for war purposes.
  • 2016: Hanson Robotics created Sophia, the first "robot citizen."
  • 2017: Facebook programmed two AI chatbots to converse and learn negotiation.
  • 2018: Alibaba's language-processing AI beat human intellect on a Stanford reading and comprehension test.
  • 2019: Google's AlphaStar reached Grandmaster in StarCraft 2.
  • 2020: OpenAI began beta testing GPT-3.
  • 2021: OpenAI developed DALL-E.

AI Capabilities

Narrow AI (Weak AI)

Specialized AI excels in specific tasks like facial recognition and autonomous driving, enhancing user experiences.

General AI (Strong AI)

Versatile AI autonomously tackles new tasks, revolutionizing industries with human-like cognitive abilities.

Superintelligent AI

Machines surpass human intelligence, driving groundbreaking innovation and igniting curiosity.

Based on Functionalities

Reactive Machines

AI that analyzes and responds without memory, exemplified by IBM's Deep Blue.

Limited Memory

AI that improves decisions using past data, seen in chatbots and self-driving cars.

Theory of Mind

Advanced AI aims to understand human emotions and beliefs for nuanced decision-making.

Based on Technologies

Machine Learning (ML)

AI systems capable of self-improvement through experience, without direct programming. They focus on creating software that can independently learn by accessing and utilizing data.

Deep Learning

A subset of ML involving many layers of neural networks. It learns from large amounts of data and powers voice control in consumer devices, image recognition, and other applications.

Natural Language Processing (NLP)

This AI technology enables machines to understand and interpret human language. It's used in chatbots, translation services, and sentiment analysis applications.

Robotics

This field involves designing, constructing, operating, and using robots and computer systems for controlling them, sensory feedback, and information processing.

Computer Vision

This technology allows machines to interpret the world visually, used in medical image analysis, surveillance, and manufacturing.

Expert Systems

AI systems that answer questions and solve problems in a specific domain of expertise using rule-based systems.

Advantages

  • Eliminates human error and risk
  • 24/7 availability
  • Unbiased decision making
  • Handles repetitive jobs
  • Cost reduction
  • Data acquisition and analysis

Disadvantages

  • Costly implementation
  • Lack of emotion and creativity
  • Degradation
  • No improvement with experience
  • Reduced jobs for humans
  • Ethical problems

FAQ

An AI model is a mathematical model used to make predictions or decisions. Some of the common types of AI models include:

  • Linear regression
  • Logistic regression
  • Decision trees
  • Neural networks

AI systems work by combining large amounts of data with intelligent algorithms — series of instructions — that allow the software to learn from patterns and features of the data.

The father of AI is John McCarthy. He is a computer scientist who coined the term "artificial intelligence" in 1955. McCarthy is also credited with developing the first AI programming language, Lisp.

It's very hard to predict. Most experts think we are still very far away from superintelligent AI systems that vastly surpass human intelligence on all tasks.

ChatGPT is one of the most popular AI apps. It ranks at #1 in Productivity and #6 in Free Apps on the App Store as of July 2023.

AI learns through data-driven processes. In machine learning, AI models are trained on data, adjusting their parameters to make predictions or decisions. Reinforcement learning uses feedback from interactions with an environment to improve over time.

AI automates certain tasks, which can lead to shifts in job roles. While some jobs may be automated, AI also creates new roles and opportunities in AI development, maintenance, and oversight.

Current AI is not conscious and does not possess emotions or thoughts like humans. AI mimics human-like responses but lacks genuine consciousness.

AI safety is a priority. Researchers are developing methods to ensure AI systems operate safely, ethically, and with human oversight.

Some key ethical issues include privacy, bias, transparency, accountability, and the moral/legal status of highly advanced AI systems. Developing ethical AI guidelines is an active area of research.
